Sense and Sensibility

Colonel Brandon

After my Junior year my school unfortunately lost both its directors; one to retirement, and the other to higher ambitions. However, good came of that for me, because my older sister, who was coming to the area after finishing her Master’s, got one of the jobs, and directed the High School play Sense and Sensibility. It was an interesting experience being directed by my older sister, though, as the second youngest of a family of five children, it wasn’t altogether foreign to me. The play was a much less intense experience for me, especially since, although I got the role of Colonel Brandon, one of the major men’s roles, I wasn’t in many of the scenes, and so was excused from many of the rehearsals. The production didn’t have the same pizazz, and excitement of Curtains (how could it, it was simply a play), but it required much more subtlety and emotional layers on my part as an actor, than Curtains, or even the other plays I have done, ever did. Righteous indignation restrained by a sense of propriety and duty, is much harder to accurately convey than the giddy excitement of Curtains’ dance numbers, or the nervous cowardice of Lester in Larceny and Old Lace. This difficulty is partly due to the increased subtlety of the emotion, but I think it is also due to the complete absence of the notions of propriety and duty in today’s society. I largely had to travel back in time, to try to imagine what it would have been like to live in Jane Austen’s world, and to be an upright gentleman abiding by these principles. This was definitely a challenge for me, though it was one that I enjoyed.

Curtains

Bobby

One of the things I looked forward to most about moving to a bigger school after my Sophomore year, was participating in the theatre program. Unfortunately I was unable to participate in the fall play my Junior year because of conflicts I had in my schedule, but I was incredibly excited to participate in their spring musical. The audition was a rather daunting experience, as it was the first rather official audition I had experienced. Even though I had not danced in a production since fifth grade or so, I received the role of Bobby Peppers, the dance choreographer for the musical within the musical. Following the audition was the two most intense months of my life. Everyday after school from three to six, we’d rehearse. I would leave rehearsal, eat out at a nearby restaurant, do some homework, as I waited for traffic to die down, and then get home at eight or so in the evening. I’d spend the next hour or two either doing homework or running through the dances again and again, and then I’d go straight to bed. I did that day after day, for two straight months, with hardly a break or rest at all. The rehearsals alone took up one hundred and seventy nine hours in all, not including the countless hours I spent outside of rehearsal practicing. These two incredibly intense months were concluded by the tech week of the most complex show I’ve ever been apart of. Rehearsals went from three to ten every day after school. In these rehearsals we’d run through the entire show twice, which involved intense dance numbers, as well as lightning fast quick changes, and constant 110% energy and attention. To make things even better, by the end of the week I had fallen sick, and soon the rest of the cast had as well. We performed six total performances hyped up on cold medicine, as we tried to hide our coughs, and moments of nausea, and the audience loved it. We packed the audience for almost all six performances, and even had some people come again and again. We were nominated for the Rita Moreno High School Musical Awards, and performed a medley from our show at the San Jose Center for the Performing Arts. Our Christopher Belling (Andy Arledge) was nominated for the Best Supporting Actor award, our Frank Cioffi (Christopher Rubingh) was nominated for the Best Leading Actor award, and our Bambi (Alyssa Hall) won the Best Supporting Actress award. The whole thing was an incredible experience, and I loved every moment of it.
